\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}

This thesis investigated semantically controlled video generation for autonomous driving using latent diffusion models. The central objective was to replace weak or sparse control interfaces with dense semantic segmentation maps that describe scene layout at pixel level while remaining interpretable for analysis and downstream use. To achieve this objective, a two-stage framework was developed in which Stage~1 predicts future semantic segmentation maps and Stage~2 translates semantic control into photorealistic RGB video.

\paragraph{Semantic-Native VAE.}
A novel Variational Autoencoder architecture was designed to bridge the gap between discrete semantic label maps and the continuous latent space of a pretrained Stable Video Diffusion model. By bypassing the 3-channel RGB bottleneck and introducing trainable Semantic Stem (19$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow$128 channels) and Semantic Head (128$\rightarrow$64$\rightarrow$19 channels) modules around a frozen VAE core, the model achieves 89.7\% mean Intersection-over-Union on KITTI-360 validation data with only $\sim$200K trainable parameters. This represents a 35.4\% absolute improvement over the naive RGB baseline approach.

\paragraph{Stage~1 Semantic Video Prediction.}
The first diffusion stage demonstrates that future semantic structure can be predicted from an initial RGB observation, but only at coarse scene level. The best Stage~1 configuration reaches 40.01\% mIoU on 170 KITTI-360 validation clips and reproduces large static regions such as road, building, sky, and vegetation reliably. However, the class-wise analysis in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} shows that thin, rare, and strongly dynamic classes remain weakly modelled. Stage~1 therefore functions as a structured semantic scene predictor rather than as a balanced semantic forecaster across all categories.

\paragraph{Stage~2 Semantic-to-Video Generation.}
The second diffusion stage confirms that semantic segmentation maps provide an effective control signal for photorealistic video generation. The strongest configuration, which combines ControlNet conditioning with partial adaptation of the video diffusion backbone, achieves 39.17\% DRN-based semantic fidelity, 85.34\% pixel accuracy, and 75.31\% frequency-weighted IoU on 487 non-overlapping KITTI-360 validation clips. A multi-scale semantic reinjection variant further improves FID and FVD to 20.9585 and 234.3064 respectively, but lowers semantic fidelity to 35.62\% mIoU. This result is important because it exposes a central trade-off of the current Stage~2 design: realism and temporal smoothness can improve even when semantic recoverability deteriorates.

\paragraph{Overall Thesis Finding.}
Taken together, the experiments show that semantic maps are a viable and interpretable control interface for autonomous driving video generation, but that precise controllability is constrained by temporal reasoning and spatial resolution. Stage~1 and Stage~2 contribute semantic loss of comparable magnitude, which means that neither component can be treated as a solved preprocessing step for the other. The main scientific contribution of the thesis is therefore twofold: first, it establishes a practical latent interface for semantic conditioning through the Semantic VAE; second, it demonstrates that the remaining bottlenecks of semantically controlled video generation lie in temporal conditioning, fine-grained structure preservation, and robust transfer of semantic control into RGB appearance.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\paragraph{Improved VAE Architecture.}
Exploring more sophisticated Semantic VAE designs---such as multi-scale stem and head modules, attention-based boundary refinement, or class-conditional normalization layers---could further improve semantic reconstruction quality, particularly for rare and small classes.

\paragraph{Motion-Aware Semantic Prediction.}
The most immediate Stage~1 extension is to strengthen temporal observability. The present formulation relies heavily on the initial frame and boundary-conditioned semantic anchors, which biases prediction toward scene persistence. Future work should therefore incorporate richer motion-aware conditioning, such as multiple initial RGB frames, ego-motion, optical flow, or depth, and should report performance in a strict frame-0-only setting in addition to the current boundary-conditioned evaluation. This would make the semantic prediction capability more faithful to the intended forecasting task.

\paragraph{Stronger Temporal and Multi-Scale Semantic Control.}
The main Stage~2 research direction is to replace frame-wise semantic entry with a genuinely spatio-temporal semantic conditioner. A temporal semantic encoder, combined with higher-resolution or hierarchical semantic pathways, would be better matched to the failure cases observed at turns, junctions, and vegetation-dominated scenes. Future variants could also combine coarse ControlNet-style residual guidance with adapter-like high-resolution control paths and explicit temporal conditioning, following the broader direction of controllable diffusion models~\cite{zhang2023adding,mou2024t2i,hu2023videocontrolnet}. Such a design would target the current realism-versus-controllability trade-off more directly than a larger denoising backbone alone.

\paragraph{End-to-End Robustness and Evaluation.}
The current Stage~2 evaluation is conducted with ground-truth semantic conditioning and therefore represents an upper bound on end-to-end performance. A more complete next step is to train and evaluate Stage~2 with Stage~1 predictions, ideally together with uncertainty estimates for the semantic control signal. In parallel, the utility of the generated data should be assessed through downstream perception tasks, such as semantic segmentation or object detection trained with synthetic data and tested on real benchmarks. This would provide a more application-oriented measure of controllability than image-level or video-level perceptual metrics alone.

\paragraph{Scalability, Generalisation, and Efficiency.}
Several practical extensions remain open. Training at higher spatial resolution would improve fine structure and object boundaries, while longer sequence generation would require either autoregressive rollout or a backbone designed for larger temporal context. Generalisation beyond KITTI-360 should be studied with additional urban-driving datasets, including Cityscapes~\cite{cordts2016cityscapes}, to test robustness across scene statistics and camera setups. Finally, faster inference through diffusion distillation or consistency-style acceleration would be necessary for interactive use or large-scale data generation.
