\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Hardware and Software}
\label{subsec:hardware}

All experiments were conducted on a compute cluster managed by SLURM, using NVIDIA GPUs with up to 48~GB VRAM (A6000 / A5000 class). The software stack includes PyTorch~2.x with CUDA~12.1, the HuggingFace Diffusers library~\cite{von-platen-etal-2022-diffusers} for diffusion model components, and Weights~\&~Biases for experiment tracking and visualization. Training was performed in mixed precision (FP16) with gradient checkpointing enabled to reduce GPU memory consumption.

\section{Semantic VAE Evaluation}
\label{sec:vae_results}

The Semantic VAE is evaluated on 100 held-out validation clips from the KITTI-360 dataset using mIoU and pixel accuracy as primary metrics. Results are reported at the end of training, after early stopping on validation mIoU.

\subsection{Architecture Development}
\label{subsec:vae_phases}

The Semantic VAE was developed through iterative refinement across three phases, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:vae_phases}.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \caption{Semantic VAE development phases and results (100 validation samples)}
    \label{tab:vae_phases}
    \begin{tabular}{llccr}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Phase} & \textbf{Approach} & \textbf{mIoU} & \textbf{Pixel Acc.} & \textbf{Trainable Params} \\
        \midrule
        1 & RGB VAE baseline (no training) & 54.3\% & 97.7\% & 0 \\
        2a & Adapter (no boundary weight) & 64.8\% & 93.8\% & 14K \\
        2a & Adapter ($\alpha=8.0$) & 79.5\% & 99.3\% & 14K \\
        2b & Native (CE only, $\alpha=4.0$) & 88.0\% & 99.1\% & 200K \\
        \textbf{2b} & \textbf{Native ($\alpha=4.0$, $\lambda=0.5$)} & \textbf{89.7\%} & \textbf{99.0\%} & \textbf{200K} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Phase~1: RGB VAE Baseline.}
Semantic IDs were converted to an RGB color palette, passed through the frozen SVD VAE, and decoded back via nearest-neighbor color matching. The contrast between the 97.7\% pixel accuracy and the 54.3\% mIoU exposes a fundamental limitation: the pretrained VAE's smooth latent space destroys semantic boundaries, which account for less than 10\% of all pixels but contribute to over 40\% of errors. Large homogeneous regions (road, building, vegetation) are reconstructed accurately, while thin structures and class transitions are severely degraded.

\paragraph{Phase~2a: Adapter Training.}
Lightweight adapter modules — comprising a $1 \times 1$ convolution from 19 to 3 channels at the encoder input and 3 to 19 channels at the decoder output, totalling 14K trainable parameters — were trained around the frozen VAE. Without boundary weighting, the adapter achieved 64.8\% mIoU, while pixel accuracy fell to 93.8\%, below the Phase~1 baseline of 97.7\%, attributable to instabilities introduced by the narrow 3-channel bottleneck. Introducing boundary-weighted cross-entropy ($\alpha=8.0$) raised mIoU substantially to 79.5\%, an absolute gain of 22.7 percentage points, with pixel accuracy recovering to 99.3\%.

\paragraph{Phase~2b: Semantic-Native Architecture.}
Removing the 3-channel RGB bottleneck by directly feeding 128-channel semantic features into the VAE core further improved performance to 88.0\% mIoU (with CE loss only). Adding Dice loss ($\lambda_{\text{Dice}}=0.5$) yielded the final best result of \textbf{89.7\% mIoU}. The 35.4\% absolute improvement from baseline to final model validates both architectural and loss function innovations.

\section{Stage~2: Semantic-to-Video Generation}
\label{sec:stage2_results}

Stage~2 is evaluated using two complementary protocols applied to held-out KITTI-360 validation data. Image and video quality metrics — Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)~\cite{heusel2017gans}, Fréchet Video Distance (FVD)~\cite{unterthiner2019fvd}, Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)~\cite{zhang2018unreasonable}, Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)~\cite{wang2004image}, and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) — are computed on 487 held-out validation clips (25 frames each, $192 \times 704$ resolution). Semantic fidelity is assessed on 475 generated frames from 19 specific clip groups (25 frames each) that match the fixed evaluation set used by the DRN-D-105 reference benchmark~\cite{yu2017dilated}. For each generated frame, the DRN-D-105 segmentation network predicts per-pixel class labels, which are compared against ground-truth KITTI-360 semantic annotations using a global $19 \times 19$ confusion matrix that accumulates confidence-weighted pixel counts across all 475 frames. The confusion matrix is updated using the per-pixel confidence maps provided by KITTI-360, and the DRN inference applies multi-scale prediction across six input scales (0.5$\times$, 0.75$\times$, 1.0$\times$, 1.25$\times$, 1.5$\times$, 1.75$\times$), with logit maps from all scales summed before the final argmax prediction. The mIoU is computed as the mean of per-class IoU values over all classes present in the evaluation set ($\text{nanmean}$ over non-NaN entries). This protocol matches the \texttt{drn\_d\_105\_MIoU} evaluation methodology, enabling a direct and fair comparison with the reference benchmark.

\subsection{Architectural Refinement: UNet Partial Unfreezing and Multi-Scale Reinjection}
\label{subsec:unet_unfreeze}

Analysis of the frozen-UNet baseline identified a fundamental bottleneck in the Stage~2 conditioning pathway. In that configuration, ControlNet residuals were injected into the UNet's intermediate representations, but the frozen decoder layers could not adapt to honour these conditioning signals. The decoder instead reverted to SVD's unconditional motion and appearance priors, suppressing semantic controllability. Additionally, the Semantic VAE latents and the noisy RGB latents operated in incompatible magnitude ranges, further diminishing the effective contribution of the ControlNet residuals.

To address this, we selectively unfroze a minimal, high-impact subset of the UNet decoder: the bottleneck mid-block — where the primary ControlNet residuals are injected at the lowest spatial resolution — together with the final normalisation and output projection layers that map the decoder's feature channels back to the 4-channel latent space. This subset corresponds to approximately 15--20\% of total UNet parameters. The UNet encoder and all decoder up-sampling blocks remained frozen to preserve SVD's spatial and temporal priors and to stay within GPU memory constraints (48~GB VRAM). The unfrozen parameters were trained at a reduced learning rate ($5 \times 10^{-6}$) relative to the ControlNet ($1 \times 10^{-5}$) to prevent catastrophic forgetting. The Semantic VAE latent scaling factor was also applied to bring the conditioning and noisy RGB latents into compatible magnitude ranges.

Table~\ref{tab:unet_unfreeze_comparison} quantifies the improvement in semantic fidelity resulting from this intervention.

All other metrics are computed on 487 validation clips under the standard single-scale protocol.}
    \label{tab:unet_unfreeze_comparison}

        \toprule

                        & \textbf{step 96,100 / 150 clips} & \textbf{step 32,700} \\

Under the multi-scale, confidence-weighted evaluation protocol, the intervention yields a gain of $+27.44$ percentage points in mIoU (23.20\% $\rightarrow$ 50.64\%), the single largest improvement observed across all training experiments in this work. Notably, the partially unfrozen model reaches this performance at step~32,700, fewer than half the steps required by the frozen baseline (96,100), confirming that the architectural change has an immediate and high-magnitude effect on gradient signal quality. Small and rare classes (truck, bus) drop to 0.00\% IoU, a limitation attributed to the $24 \times 88$ effective latent resolution and the restricted evaluation set of 475 frames covering 15 of 19 semantic classes; the remaining four classes (traffic light, rider, train, bicycle) are absent from the 19 evaluation clip groups and therefore excluded from the mIoU computation.

We subsequently extended this configuration with a multi-scale semantic reinjection mechanism inside the ControlNet encoder. Instead of injecting the semantic latent only once at the input, the same latent representation is resized to each intermediate encoder resolution and added again after each non-final down-sampling stage through zero-initialised projections. At the training resolution of $192 \times 704$, this refreshes semantic context at latent resolutions of $12 \times 44$, $6 \times 22$, and $3 \times 11$ while preserving the pretrained feature path at initialisation. On the 487 validation clips, this variant improves the distributional and temporal quality metrics, reducing FID from 21.9122 to 20.9585 and FVD-I3D from 255.2064 to 234.3064. However, the DRN-based semantic fidelity under the same multi-scale evaluation protocol decreases from 50.64\% to 43.69\% mIoU, with corresponding drops in pixel accuracy, mean class accuracy, and frequency-weighted IoU. This result indicates a clear trade-off: multi-scale reinjection improves global realism and temporal consistency, but the current training configuration does not yet preserve semantic boundaries as effectively as the plain UNet-unfreeze model.

However, LPIPS increases from 0.3569 to 0.3744, while SSIM and PSNR decrease from 0.4426 and 14.6185~dB to 0.4140 and 13.8176~dB respectively. This suggests that multi-scale reinjection improves global realism and temporal consistency, but does not improve frame-wise similarity to the paired ground-truth video. Distributional measures such as FID and FVD are therefore informative complements to pairwise fidelity measures in this setting.

Table~\ref{tab:semantic_fidelity} shows that the DRN-based semantic fidelity metric favours the plain UNet-unfreeze model. The multi-scale reinjection variant reduces mIoU from 50.64\% to 43.69\%, pixel accuracy from 85.34\% to 83.41\%, mean class accuracy from 50.88\% to 46.63\%, and frequency-weighted IoU from 75.31\% to 72.61\%. Because this evaluation measures semantic consistency through an external segmentation network, the decrease indicates that the current reinjection variant does not yet translate its additional semantic cues into more recoverable class structure. The gap between pixel accuracy and mIoU in both configurations reflects the difficulty of generating rare or small-scale classes, which contribute equally to mIoU despite their limited spatial extent.

Car achieves the highest per-class IoU at 92.17\%, driven by its large surface coverage and visually distinctive texture.

Moderately covered classes present intermediate results. Terrain (57.39\%) and sidewalk (56.81\%) benefit from large surface areas, while fence (53.18\%) and wall (50.73\%) depend on boundary-precise generation. Traffic sign (42.12\%) and pole (35.02\%) represent thin or elevated structures that are inherently challenging to render accurately at the $24 \times 88$ latent resolution.

These limitations are consistent with the Stage~1 analysis and point to a shared bottleneck: fine-grained and rare-class generation requires higher latent resolution and broader evaluation coverage than the current setting provides.

\subsection{Comparison with Reference Methods}
\label{subsec:stage2_reference_comparison}

The comparison must therefore be interpreted cautiously. The methods differ in conditioning modality, architecture, spatial resolution, dataset, and evaluation protocol. In particular, our Stage~2 is evaluated under ground-truth semantic conditioning, so the teacher-forced Box2Video setting of Ctrl-V is the closest analogue within that framework because both assume access to a known control sequence for all generated frames.

The comparison highlights two complementary observations. Relative to SVS-GAN, the UNet-unfreeze model attains slightly lower DRN-based semantic fidelity (50.64\% vs.\ 53.89\%), but a substantially lower FVD (255.2064 vs.\ 1103.69). Its FID of 21.9122 is also slightly higher than the SVS-GAN value of 19.63. This indicates that the proposed diffusion-based Stage~2 remains competitive with the strongest dense semantic baseline, but does not yet establish the highest DRN-based semantic fidelity among the compared methods.

The comparison with Ctrl-V should be interpreted differently. Ctrl-V is not a dense semantic baseline, but a parent framework using sparse bounding-box control on KITTI. Even so, the DRN-based Ctrl-V comparison score of 30.48\% mIoU remains well below the 50.64\% achieved by the UNet-unfreeze model, although direct benchmarking is limited by the different dataset and control modality. This suggests that dense semantic control can yield substantially higher recoverable scene structure than sparse object-level control under the reported DRN-based comparison, but it also reinforces the central challenge of this thesis: scene-level specificity increases the burden on the renderer to preserve fine-grained structure across the full image. The reinjection variant further improves FID and FVD, but its 43.69\% mIoU falls below both SVS-GAN and the plain UNet-unfreeze configuration.

The strong diagonal entries for road, building, vegetation, sky, and car confirm that these classes are generated with high visual fidelity. Several systematic off-diagonal patterns are visible and provide insight into the generation characteristics of the model. Sidewalk and road are mutually confused due to their shared surface texture and spatial adjacency; boundary-precise generation at the operating resolution is insufficient to consistently separate them. Thin pole structures are frequently absorbed into surrounding building pixels, reflecting the difficulty of rendering narrow vertical elements that may span only a few pixels in the generated frame. The rider class is confused with person, as these categories share similar visual appearance and differ primarily in the presence of an associated vehicle. Fence pixels show notable confusion with vegetation, consistent with their frequent spatial co-occurrence in suburban scenes. Traffic signs are partially subsumed by surrounding pole and vegetation context, highlighting the challenge of generating small elevated objects against complex backgrounds. These patterns collectively represent limitations of fine-grained detail generation at the operating resolution rather than fundamental failures in semantic conditioning.

\section{Cross-Stage Discussion}
\label{sec:cross_stage}

Stage~1 achieves a direct semantic prediction mIoU of 40.01\% (170 validation clips, single-scale evaluation), while the best DRN-based Stage~2 configuration, the UNet-unfreeze model, achieves 50.64\% mIoU as measured by re-segmenting the generated RGB frames with DRN-D-105 using the multi-scale, confidence-weighted protocol on 475 frames with ground-truth semantic conditioning. These two figures are not directly comparable because they use different evaluation protocols: Stage~1 uses direct label-space comparison over a larger set of 4{,}250 frames, while Stage~2 uses multi-scale DRN inference with confidence weighting over 475 specifically selected frames matching the reference benchmark evaluation set. Despite this protocol difference, the Stage~2 result demonstrates that the RGB generation stage, under ideal ground-truth semantic conditioning, reproduces semantic structure that a strong DRN model can recover at a level that exceeds the DRN-based Ctrl-V comparison score and remains close to the SVS-GAN reference. The Stage~2 semantic fidelity metric remains an upper bound on full pipeline performance: in practice, Stage~2 receives Stage~1 predictions rather than ground-truth maps, and any semantic errors introduced in Stage~1 propagate through to the RGB generation. Advances in Stage~1 prediction accuracy would therefore directly reduce the semantic noise entering Stage~2, while advances in Stage~2 controllability would improve the fidelity with which a given semantic map — whether ground-truth or predicted — is translated into realistic video. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} examines these directions in the context of the broader framework limitations.
