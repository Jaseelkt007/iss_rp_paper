\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Hardware and Software}
\label{subsec:hardware}

All experiments were conducted on a compute cluster managed by SLURM, using NVIDIA GPUs with up to 48~GB VRAM (A6000 / A5000 class). The software stack includes PyTorch~2.x with CUDA~12.1, the HuggingFace Diffusers library~\cite{von-platen-etal-2022-diffusers} for diffusion model components, and Weights~\&~Biases for experiment tracking and visualization. Training was performed in mixed precision (FP16) with gradient checkpointing enabled to reduce GPU memory consumption.

\section{Semantic VAE Evaluation}
\label{sec:vae_results}

The Semantic VAE is evaluated on 100 held-out validation clips from the KITTI-360 dataset using mIoU and pixel accuracy as primary metrics. Results are reported at the end of training, after early stopping on validation mIoU.

\subsection{Architecture Development}
\label{subsec:vae_phases}

The Semantic VAE was developed through iterative refinement across three phases, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:vae_phases}.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \caption{Semantic VAE development phases and results (100 validation samples)}
    \label{tab:vae_phases}
    \begin{tabular}{llccr}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Phase} & \textbf{Approach} & \textbf{mIoU} & \textbf{Pixel Acc.} & \textbf{Trainable Params} \\
        \midrule
        1 & RGB VAE baseline (no training) & 54.3\% & 97.7\% & 0 \\
        2a & Adapter (no boundary weight) & 64.8\% & 93.8\% & 14K \\
        2a & Adapter ($\alpha=8.0$) & 79.5\% & 99.3\% & 14K \\
        2b & Native (CE only, $\alpha=4.0$) & 88.0\% & 99.1\% & 200K \\
        \textbf{2b} & \textbf{Native ($\alpha=4.0$, $\lambda=0.5$)} & \textbf{89.7\%} & \textbf{99.0\%} & \textbf{200K} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Phase~1: RGB VAE Baseline.}
Semantic IDs were converted to an RGB color palette, passed through the frozen SVD VAE, and decoded back via nearest-neighbor color matching. The contrast between the 97.7\% pixel accuracy and the 54.3\% mIoU exposes a fundamental limitation: the pretrained VAE's smooth latent space destroys semantic boundaries, which account for less than 10\% of all pixels but contribute to over 40\% of errors. Large homogeneous regions (road, building, vegetation) are reconstructed accurately, while thin structures and class transitions are severely degraded.

\paragraph{Phase~2a: Adapter Training.}
Lightweight adapter modules — comprising a $1 \times 1$ convolution from 19 to 3 channels at the encoder input and 3 to 19 channels at the decoder output, totalling 14K trainable parameters — were trained around the frozen VAE. Without boundary weighting, the adapter achieved 64.8\% mIoU, while pixel accuracy fell to 93.8\%, below the Phase~1 baseline of 97.7\%, attributable to instabilities introduced by the narrow 3-channel bottleneck. Introducing boundary-weighted cross-entropy ($\alpha=8.0$) raised mIoU substantially to 79.5\%, an absolute gain of 22.7 percentage points, with pixel accuracy recovering to 99.3\%.

\paragraph{Phase~2b: Semantic-Native Architecture.}
Removing the 3-channel RGB bottleneck by directly feeding 128-channel semantic features into the VAE core further improved performance to 88.0\% mIoU (with CE loss only). Adding Dice loss ($\lambda_{\text{Dice}}=0.5$) yielded the final best result of \textbf{89.7\% mIoU}. The 35.4\% absolute improvement from baseline to final model validates both architectural and loss function innovations.

\section{Stage~2: Semantic-to-Video Generation}
\label{sec:stage2_results}

Stage~2 is evaluated on 487 held-out validation clips from KITTI-360 (25 frames each, $192 \times 704$ resolution). For each clip, the initial RGB frame provides appearance conditioning via the CLIP encoder and RGB VAE, while ground-truth semantic segmentation maps provide structural conditioning via the Semantic VAE. Generation uses 30 denoising steps with a linearly increasing guidance scale from 1.0 to 3.0. Two complementary evaluation protocols are applied: distributional and perceptual image quality metrics — Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)~\cite{heusel2017gans}, Fréchet Video Distance (FVD)~\cite{unterthiner2019fvd}, Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)~\cite{zhang2018unreasonable}, Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)~\cite{wang2004image}, and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) — are computed against ground-truth RGB frames; and semantic fidelity is assessed by applying an independently trained DRN-D-105 segmentation network~\cite{yu2017dilated} to the generated frames and comparing the predicted segmentation against the ground-truth labels using mIoU and pixel accuracy.

\subsection{Architectural Refinement: UNet Partial Unfreezing}
\label{subsec:unet_unfreeze}

Analysis of the frozen-UNet baseline identified a fundamental bottleneck in the Stage~2 conditioning pathway. In that configuration, ControlNet residuals were injected into the UNet's intermediate representations, but the frozen decoder layers could not adapt to honour these conditioning signals. The decoder instead reverted to SVD's unconditional motion and appearance priors, suppressing semantic controllability. Additionally, the Semantic VAE latents and the noisy RGB latents operated in incompatible magnitude ranges, further diminishing the effective contribution of the ControlNet residuals.

To address this, we selectively unfroze a minimal, high-impact subset of the UNet decoder: the bottleneck mid-block — where the primary ControlNet residuals are injected at the lowest spatial resolution — together with the final normalisation and output projection layers that map the decoder's feature channels back to the 4-channel latent space. This subset corresponds to approximately 15--20\% of total UNet parameters. The UNet encoder and all decoder up-sampling blocks remained frozen to preserve SVD's spatial and temporal priors and to stay within GPU memory constraints (48~GB VRAM). The unfrozen parameters were trained at a reduced learning rate ($5 \times 10^{-6}$) relative to the ControlNet ($1 \times 10^{-5}$) to prevent catastrophic forgetting. The Semantic VAE latent scaling factor was also applied to bring the conditioning and noisy RGB latents into compatible magnitude ranges.

Table~\ref{tab:unet_unfreeze_comparison} quantifies the improvement in semantic fidelity resulting from this intervention.

\begin{table}[t]
    \centering
    \caption{Stage~2 semantic fidelity: frozen UNet baseline vs.\ UNet partial unfreezing (DRN-D-105, KITTI-360 validation set)}
    \label{tab:unet_unfreeze_comparison}
    \begin{tabular}{lcc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Frozen UNet} & \textbf{UNet Unfreeze} \\
                        & \textbf{step 96,100 / 150 clips} & \textbf{step 32,700 / 487 clips} \\
        \midrule
        mIoU            & 23.20\% & \textbf{39.17\%} \\
        Pixel Accuracy  & 67.63\% & \textbf{85.34\%} \\

        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The intervention yields a gain of $+15.97$ percentage points in mIoU (23.20\% $\rightarrow$ 39.17\%), the single largest improvement observed across all training experiments in this work. Dominant classes benefit substantially: car improves from 40.61\% to 84.29\% IoU, sky from 55.11\% to 84.37\%, and road from 70.66\% to 85.17\%. Notably, the partially unfrozen model reaches this performance at step~32,700 — fewer than half the steps required by the frozen baseline (96,100) — confirming that the architectural change has an immediate and high-magnitude effect on gradient signal quality. Small and rare classes (traffic light, bicycle, train) remain near zero, a limitation attributed to the $24 \times 88$ effective latent resolution rather than training capacity. These residual gaps motivate the multi-scale conditioning extension described in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

The per-class results follow the same frequency-dependent pattern observed in Stage~1. Spatially dominant classes with large contiguous regions — road (85.17\%), sky (84.37\%), car (84.29\%), vegetation (80.62\%), and building (75.25\%) — are generated with high fidelity, confirming that ControlNet conditioning effectively translates large-scale semantic structure into photorealistic appearance. Car achieves particularly high IoU at 84.29\%, driven by its large surface coverage and visually distinctive texture.

Moderately covered classes present intermediate results. Terrain (59.06\%) and sidewalk (55.20\%) benefit from large surface areas, while wall (47.63\%) and fence (37.74\%) depend on boundary-precise generation. Traffic sign (30.99\%) and pole (25.67\%) represent thin or elevated structures that are inherently challenging to render accurately at the $24 \times 88$ latent resolution.

Rare and small-scale classes remain the primary limitation. Rider (9.88\%), bus (7.34\%), motorcycle (5.17\%), bicycle (3.15\%), and train (0.32\%) are severely constrained by class rarity and limited spatial extent in the latent space. Traffic light achieves 0.00\% IoU, occupying fewer than 341 ground-truth pixels across the entire evaluation set — effectively below the latent-space resolution threshold. These limitations are consistent with the Stage~1 analysis and point to a shared bottleneck: fine-grained class generation requires higher latent resolution than the current $24 \times 88$ provides.

\section{Cross-Stage Discussion}
\label{sec:cross_stage}

Stage~1 achieves a direct semantic prediction mIoU of 40.01\% (170 clips), while Stage~2 achieves 39.17\% mIoU as measured by re-segmenting the generated RGB frames with DRN-D-105 (487 clips with ground-truth semantic conditioning). The numerical proximity of these two figures warrants careful interpretation. The Stage~2 semantic fidelity metric is evaluated under an idealized condition: ground-truth semantic maps are supplied as conditioning input, which represents an upper bound on Stage~2 performance in the full end-to-end pipeline. In practice, Stage~2 receives Stage~1 predictions rather than ground-truth maps, and any semantic errors introduced in Stage~1 propagate through to the RGB generation. The similarity in mIoU therefore does not imply equivalent capability; rather, it suggests that both stages contribute to the same order-of-magnitude of semantic fidelity loss, making improvements to either stage directly beneficial for end-to-end performance. Advances in Stage~1 prediction accuracy — for instance, through longer training or stronger temporal attention — would reduce the semantic noise entering Stage~2. Advances in Stage~2 controllability — through multi-scale ControlNet conditioning or higher-resolution latent representations — would improve the fidelity with which a given semantic map is translated into realistic video. Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion} examines these directions in the context of the broader framework limitations.
