\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

This chapter analyzes the Stage~1 experimental results, examines the architectural limitations of semantic video prediction, discusses how the current evaluation protocol should be interpreted, and outlines the most justified next design directions.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Analysis of Experimental Results}
\label{sec:result_analysis}

\subsection{Semantic VAE Performance}
\label{subsec:vae_analysis}

The progressive development of the Semantic VAE from Phase~1 (54.3\% mIoU) to Phase~2b (89.7\% mIoU) reveals several important insights about encoding discrete semantic information through continuous latent spaces.

\paragraph{The RGB Bottleneck.}
The most significant architectural decision was eliminating the 3-channel RGB bottleneck. The Phase~1 baseline demonstrates that a pretrained RGB VAE, despite achieving 97.7\% pixel accuracy, fundamentally cannot preserve semantic boundaries---the 35.4\% gap between Phase~1 and Phase~2b mIoU is almost entirely attributable to boundary errors. This finding has implications beyond semantic VAEs: any application that requires preserving discrete or sharp features through a VAE designed for natural images will encounter similar degradation.

The underlying cause is the VAE's latent space topology. During pretraining on natural images, the VAE learns to encode smooth, continuous signals. The latent manifold is optimized for perceptual reconstruction quality (via LPIPS loss), which inherently prioritizes low-frequency content over high-frequency boundaries. When discrete label maps are forced through this bottleneck, the encoder maps adjacent but semantically different regions to nearby latent codes, and the decoder produces blended outputs at transitions.

\paragraph{Boundary Emphasis as the Key Lever.}
The +14.7\% mIoU improvement from boundary weighting alone (Phase~2a, $\alpha=0 \rightarrow \alpha=8$) confirms that the loss function plays a disproportionately important role relative to the small number of boundary pixels. Approximately 7--10\% of pixels in a typical KITTI-360 frame lie on class boundaries, yet these pixels contribute to over 40\% of mIoU errors in the unweighted case. By assigning $5\times$ weight to boundary pixels ($\alpha=4.0$), the optimization landscape is reshaped to prioritize these challenging regions.

\paragraph{Dice Loss and Class Balance.}
The Dice loss provides a more modest improvement (+1.7\% mIoU) but is critical for rare classes. Traffic signs, which occupy only $\sim$12K pixels out of $\sim$54M in the validation set (0.02\%), improve from 78.2\% to 82.2\% IoU with Dice loss. Without it, the cross-entropy gradient is dominated by the $\sim$14.6M vegetation pixels, effectively ignoring classes with three orders of magnitude fewer pixels.

\subsection{Stage~1 Semantic Prediction}
\label{subsec:stage1_discussion}

The Stage~1 results in Section~\ref{sec:stage1_results} show that the model learns the coarse structure of driving scenes, but not balanced future semantic forecasting. This interpretation becomes even clearer in a broader diagnostic evaluation of the current best checkpoint on 487 KITTI-360 validation clips of 25 frames at $192 \times 704$, where the model reaches 35.45\% mIoU, 80.13\% pixel accuracy, 45.41\% mean class accuracy, and 67.71\% frequency-weighted IoU. The comparatively high pixel accuracy together with the much lower mIoU indicates that dominant classes account for most correct predictions, while minority classes remain weakly modeled. Stage~1 therefore behaves more like a structured scene-persistence model than a balanced semantic forecaster.

Per-class IoU supports this interpretation. Large and mostly static classes remain comparatively strong, with 84.39\% for road, 67.05\% for building, 72.02\% for vegetation, and 69.43\% for sky. By contrast, thin or rare classes collapse, including 10.20\% for pole, 5.60\% for rider, 6.63\% for bus, 0.02\% for train, and 0.00\% for both traffic light and bicycle. When grouped by semantic difficulty, large static classes average 65.86\% IoU whereas thin and small classes average only 7.81\%. The error pattern is therefore not random; it follows the architectural bias of the current pipeline toward persistent, spatially extended structure.

The clip-level distribution reveals the same tendency. Across the 487 validation clips, the mean clip mIoU is 42.85\% with a standard deviation of 10.55\%, the median is 43.24\%, and the range spans from 13.49\% to 71.19\%. More importantly, some clips exhibit very high pixel accuracy despite poor mIoU, for example 24.26\% mIoU at 99.25\% pixel accuracy and 28.27\% mIoU at 97.24\% pixel accuracy. Such cases show that the model can reproduce the dominant background layout while still missing the minority classes that determine semantic completeness. This discrepancy motivates a discussion centered on conditioning, representation, and motion observability rather than on average pixel agreement alone.

\section{Architectural Limitations of Stage~1}
\label{sec:stage1_limitations}

This section examines the components of the current Stage~1 design that most plausibly explain the observed class-wise failure pattern.

\subsection{Copy-Forward Bias from Repeated Semantic Anchors}
\label{subsec:copy_forward_bias}

The strongest inductive bias in the current pipeline is temporal persistence. During denoising, the first-frame semantic representation is injected not only at the beginning of the sequence but repeatedly across the intermediate temporal slots, and an additional ground-truth semantic anchor is provided at the final frame. The easiest solution under this conditioning scheme is to preserve the initial layout unless the denoiser finds strong evidence to modify it. This behavior is beneficial for road, building, vegetation, and sky, whose spatial arrangement changes slowly, but it penalizes riders, bicycles, traffic lights, and other structures whose future state differs substantially from the first frame. The per-class IoU pattern described above matches this bias closely, indicating that the dominant limitation is not a failure to represent the scene at all, but a preference for propagating the first visible configuration forward in time.

The current error profile is unlikely to be explained only by insufficient trainable capacity. In the best-performing configuration, the denoising backbone is no longer restricted to a narrow temporal-only update regime, yet minority-class performance remains poor. This observation suggests that the main bottlenecks lie in the conditioning design and the representation itself, rather than in an overly frozen network. The Stage~1 problem is therefore better understood as a misaligned forecasting formulation than as a simple optimization deficit.

\subsection{Compression and Prior Mismatch}
\label{subsec:compression_prior}

The latent representation further amplifies this persistence bias. Semantic segmentation maps are encoded at an effective spatial resolution of $24 \times 88$, which corresponds to an $8\times$ downsampling of the $192 \times 704$ input. This compression is manageable for broad surfaces such as road or sky, but it is severe for poles, traffic lights, bicycles, or partially occluded pedestrians, which may occupy only a few pixels after resizing. Once these structures are compressed into a very small number of latent cells, subsequent denoising cannot reliably reconstruct class boundaries that were weakly represented at the input. The success of the Semantic VAE in Section~\ref{subsec:vae_analysis} shows that semantic information can be encoded effectively, but the forecasting stage still has to infer future changes after this compression has already removed much of the fine detail.

A second source of mismatch comes from the inherited RGB-video prior of the denoising backbone. The backbone was originally pretrained for natural-video latent denoising, where smooth appearance continuity is beneficial. Stage~1 instead requires categorical boundary preservation, class-balanced reasoning, and accurate updates to small moving objects. Even after fine-tuning, an RGB-oriented prior naturally favours coarse scene continuity over discrete semantic transitions. This helps explain why the model remains competent on large static regions while underperforming on minority classes whose identity is defined by sharp boundaries and local motion.

\subsection{Latent Calibration and Objective Mismatch}
\label{subsec:latent_calibration}

The latent statistics indicate an additional calibration problem. A diagnostic report on the semantic latent distribution shows a global standard deviation of 9.4974 before scaling. Reusing the RGB-latent scaling factor of 0.18215 produces a scaled standard deviation of 1.7299, whereas a semantic-specific factor of approximately 0.1053 would be required to approach unit variance. In practical terms, the semantic signal enters diffusion at a larger magnitude than the original SVD noise schedule assumes. This does not invalidate training, but it changes the effective signal-to-noise ratio and makes optimization less well aligned with the pretrained backbone.

The training objective compounds this issue. Stage~1 is optimized primarily in continuous latent space, which is suitable for preserving coarse consistency but does not directly reward class-balanced semantic correctness. Large contiguous regions therefore dominate the loss, while missing a small traffic light or bicycle contributes comparatively little to the optimization signal. The persistent gap between pixel accuracy and mIoU is consistent with this formulation. A model trained under such an objective is expected to prioritize the classes that occupy most pixels, even when the resulting semantic map is incomplete from a perception perspective.

\subsection{Insufficient Motion Information}
\label{subsec:motion_information}

The conditioning bandwidth is also too narrow for genuine motion reasoning. The model receives only a single RGB frame through a global visual embedding and does not explicitly observe ego-motion, depth, optical flow, camera pose, or multiple preceding frames. However, future semantics in driving scenes are determined not only by appearance but also by geometry and motion. Occlusion changes, turning trajectories, relative scale changes, and new object entries cannot be inferred reliably from a single static global descriptor. The current conditioning therefore encourages the model to predict the most persistent semantic arrangement compatible with the first frame, which is precisely the behavior reflected in the per-class results.

\section{Evaluation Protocol and Metric Interpretation}
\label{sec:stage1_metrics}

This section discusses how the current Stage~1 evaluation setup should be interpreted when reading the reported scores.

\subsection{What the Current Stage~1 Score Measures}
\label{subsec:stage1_protocol}

The current Stage~1 score should not be interpreted as pure prediction from the first RGB frame alone. In the present formulation, the denoiser is guided by a repeated first-frame semantic prior throughout the intermediate frames and also receives a ground-truth semantic anchor at the final frame. The resulting task is therefore better described as boundary-conditioned semantic sequence generation than as unconstrained future forecasting. This distinction is important because the end-point anchor reduces uncertainty about long-range scene evolution and makes the task easier than a strict frame-0-only setting. The current score remains useful, but it should be interpreted as measuring how well the model fills in a semantic trajectory between strong structural anchors.

This protocol choice also helps explain why the model performs best in clips with gradual scene evolution. When the future remains close to the initial layout, interpolation between anchors is sufficient to produce plausible semantics. When the scene contains new dynamic agents, thin emerging structures, or substantial ego-motion, the anchor-based formulation provides less useful guidance for intermediate frames. The current evaluation thus reveals a real capability, namely temporally coherent semantic interpolation, but it does not yet isolate the harder task of open-ended semantic forecasting.

\subsection{Why mIoU Must Be Read Together with Pixel Accuracy}
\label{subsec:stage1_metric_reading}

For Stage~1, mIoU and pixel accuracy answer different questions. Pixel accuracy reflects whether the dominant regions of the frame are classified correctly, whereas mIoU penalizes failures on minority classes much more strongly by averaging across classes. The diagnostic examples discussed above, where pixel accuracy exceeds 97\% while mIoU remains below 30\%, show that pixel accuracy alone can mask severe omissions of semantically important objects. Conversely, mIoU by itself does not distinguish whether errors arise from slight boundary shifts or complete class disappearance. A reliable assessment of Stage~1 therefore requires both metrics, together with per-class IoU, because only their combination exposes the class imbalance inherent in the current pipeline.

A further implication is that future evaluations should separate endpoint-conditioned and interior-frame performance. Reporting full-sequence scores alone overestimates the ability of the model to forecast unseen intermediate semantics, because the conditioned endpoints are part of the evaluated clip. A more transparent protocol would report both full-sequence metrics and metrics on frames~1--23 only, alongside a strict frame-0-only evaluation without a final semantic anchor. Such a split would preserve comparability while making the true forecasting difficulty explicit.

\section{Implications for Future Stage~1 Design}
\label{sec:stage1_future}

Before architectural variants are compared, the evaluation protocol should distinguish pure frame-0-only forecasting from boundary-conditioned generation and should report interior-frame performance separately. This clarification is necessary to ensure that any subsequent improvement can be attributed to better forecasting rather than stronger endpoint guidance. Once the protocol is fixed, the most justified immediate architecture change is to replace single-shot concatenative conditioning with a semantic-specific pathway that re-injects semantic information at multiple spatial resolutions. This would reduce the burden on the first latent layer to preserve all class detail throughout the denoising hierarchy and would better protect small structures whose identity is currently lost early.

Semantic-specific latent normalization should accompany this change. The current latent statistics indicate that the semantic representation does not share the variance regime of the RGB latent space for which the backbone was pretrained. Calibrating the semantic latents before diffusion would make optimization more consistent with the inherited noise schedule and would remove an avoidable source of training friction. Together, multi-scale semantic conditioning and semantic-specific latent normalization directly target the failure modes that are evidenced by the present evaluation.

Additional modalities are also necessary if the goal is accurate future semantic prediction rather than semantic persistence. Multiple RGB frames would provide short-term motion evidence, ego-motion or pose information would explain large background displacements, and dense geometric cues such as depth or optical flow would help the model reason about occlusions and small moving agents. These signals are more informative for Stage~1 than increasing model size alone, because they supply the missing information required to predict future class arrangements. Without such modalities, the network remains forced to extrapolate dynamic scene evolution from a single static observation.

Larger architectural changes should therefore be treated as a second step, not the first. A higher-resolution or hierarchical semantic latent representation would likely improve boundary preservation, and a more flexible denoiser could then exploit the richer representation more effectively. However, if stale first-frame conditioning, limited motion cues, and latent miscalibration remain unresolved, a larger backbone will inherit the same forecasting ambiguity. The central lesson from the current Stage~1 analysis is that better semantic prediction requires better information flow, not only more parameters.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This chapter has analyzed the current Stage~1 pipeline, revealing that:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The Semantic-Native VAE achieves 89.7\% mIoU through the combination of bypassing the RGB bottleneck, boundary-weighted loss, and Dice loss---each addressing a distinct failure mode of naive approaches.
    
    \item The current Stage~1 diffusion model preserves coarse scene topology but does not yet provide balanced future semantic forecasting. On 487 KITTI-360 validation clips, the diagnostic analysis yields 35.45\% mIoU and 80.13\% pixel accuracy, confirming that dominant classes are learned more reliably than thin, rare, or highly dynamic classes.
    
    \item The main bottlenecks are architectural rather than purely optimization-related. Repeated first-frame semantic anchoring, aggressive latent compression, semantic latent miscalibration, and missing motion cues together bias the model toward semantic persistence instead of accurate future structure prediction.
    
    \item The most justified next updates are protocol clarification, semantic-specific latent normalization, multi-scale semantic conditioning, and richer motion-aware conditioning. Larger denoisers or backbone replacements should be considered only after these information-flow limitations are addressed.
\end{enumerate}
